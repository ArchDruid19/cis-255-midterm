Albert Einstein

Theoretical physcisct, Philosopher, Educator.

I was born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, a small city in the German Empire (modern day Germany). By age 13 I was already extremely passionate about mathematics, teaching myself algebra, calculus, and Euclidean geometry. What fascinated me were puzzles of nature: light, magnetism, and the invisible forces that seemed to govern the world.
After studying at the Polytechnic in Zurich, I found myself working as a technical expert in the Swiss Patent Office. It was there, in the quiet hours after work, that I developed many of the ideas that would later be called revolutionary. In 1905, I published papers on the photoelectric effect, Brownian motion, and the special theory of relativity which eventually reshaped our understanding of space and time.
Later, in 1915, I completed the general theory of relativity, which revealed gravity not as a force but as the curvature of spacetime.
Beyond science, I have always cared deeply about humanity. I opposed war, nationalism, and injustice wherever I saw them. Though I never considered myself a man of politics, I could not remain silent when conscience demanded speech.
In my later years, I settled in Princeton, where I continued to think, to teach, and to wonder.

Hobbies

Advocacy

Throughout my life I have always been a pacifist. Prior to WWI, In 1914 I signed a counter manifesto that called for an end to hostilites and a United Europe. I was also part of the German League of Human Rights, and was very dedicated to repairng relations between Germany and France after the war.
After moving to America in 1932 I became involved with the civil rights movement, where I worked with a number of organizations (such as the Princeton chapter of the NAACP) to demand equality and renouce racist ideas.

Learning and Teaching

One of my greatest joys has always been the act of learning. I have devoted myself to understanding the mysteries of the world and, whenever possible, sharing that understanding with others. From a very young age, I sensed that education was not merely a path to a profession, but a lifelong endeavor that nourishes the mind and spirit. To learn is to exercise curiosity, to question what others take for granted, and to find beauty in the order hidden beneath apparent chaos. Teaching, in turn, has always been a natural extension of this pursuit, for knowledge finds its true value only when it enlightens others.

Music

Music has always been very important to me. Since the age of six I have been playing the violin.
